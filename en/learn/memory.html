Working and short-term memory, explained calmly

Working memory is the small mental workspace where you hold a phone number, a train of thought, or the start of a sentence just long enough to use it. It's short-lived by design and has a real limit — only a few items at once. Like any everyday skill, keeping things steady in that workspace gets a little easier the more you practise it.

What short daily practice can — and can't — do

With short, regular practice you reliably get better at the memory tasks themselves — holding a longer string of digits, keeping more cards in mind, losing your place less often. During and just after a session you'll usually feel sharper and more present. Researchers call this near-transfer, and it's well supported.

What it won't do is rewire your memory for good, or guard against age-related decline — the evidence simply doesn't stretch that far, so Facette doesn't promise it. You'll get genuinely good at these exercises and enjoy a calmer, more focused few minutes; that's what it's for, and it's enough.

How to practise

Keep it short and regular — five unhurried minutes most days will do more than one long, forgotten session. Stay at your edge: a sequence that's a touch longer than feels comfortable is exactly where memory stretches, as long as you can still just about manage it. And keep the room calm; holding things in mind works better when you're not racing a punishing clock.

Questions & answers

Can brain games improve my working memory?

You'll reliably get better at the trained tasks — longer digit spans, tighter recall in the games — and feel more present during a session. That's near-transfer, and it's well supported. A higher IQ or broad, lasting change isn't, so Facette doesn't claim it.

How long should I practise?

Five focused minutes a day is plenty to keep the habit and stay at your edge. Consistency matters far more than long, occasional sessions.
